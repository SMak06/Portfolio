Accommodation

Short version: Take an Accommodation through University Living which gives you meals. Don't take a flat.

The Accommodation that I lived at was located at Ladbroke Road, named "Bowden Court". I was assigned to it by a company called University Living which is like an AirBnB company but for students and it handles student accommodations. Bowden Court is a student accommodation which provides breakfast and dinner all through week days while breakfast and lunch on weekends (elaborated in Food column). The room that I stayed in was a twin sharing with 2 single beds, 2 sets of cupboards, 2 sets of drawers (there are 3 drawers in each set, one of which has a lock), one mirror attached behind the door, a washbasin, a table, 2 chairs, a radiator and a window (looked around) and a door. The tapwater is drinkable and has running water 24/7 with both cold and warm water. The beds are provided with two pillows, a bedsheet and a comforter. In India, we use heaters to keep our houses warm, here, they use centralized heating using radiators. The heat from radiator can be adjusted using a knob and usually the room is really warm even though I keep the window open 24/7 for proper ventilation. I'm sitting here in a Tshirt and a hoodie while the temperature just outside the window is (I legit checked my phone) is 6 (degree symbol). The room is spacious enough for two people. There are four floors on the building plus a ground floor and a basement. The rooms are more spacious on the 1st floor and the least on the 4th (IDK about the rooms on the ground floor). There are rooms for a single tenant as well (but I don't have any info on them).

The food is available in the canteen on the basement. It's a pretty big canteen, more like a hostel-mess. The food is served as a buffet. It's a place to interact with other students and weirdos in the accommodation. The timing and everything else is elaborated in the Food section I could link the foo... [be quiet! be quiet! make a straight face] :)

You get a number of keys and "access device" (IDK what to call it exactly). You get a key for your room door, your drawer with lock and your cupboard. The access device helps you get into the accommodation, into the main access point of rooms and into the gym. I carry the set of keys everytime I step outside the room. Boy, is it fun when your roommate forgets the keys

Additional facilities include a TV room (with really comfy sofas and beanbags), a microwave room, a gym, a laundry room and a study room (but I've never been there). The gym is meh! You get the basic machines but a lot of necessary ones are missing including the pulley. The dumbbells go upto 20kgs. (I bought a resistance band which gives upto 25kg of resistance and that worked out fine for me). Laundry costs 3.20£ for the washing machine and 0.20£ minimum for the dryer (you add more money to it to make it run longer). Sh!t's expensive round here. You can get print-outs taken at the accommodation itself, and that costs 0.25£ per page (I've merely started talking about expenses).

There are about 8-10 toilets and showers each, on every floor (except basement). Shared toilets I thought would be a problem but aren't. They are usually empty (not because there are less people but because [who cares!?] I seriously don't know and ignorance is bliss). The toilets are neat and clean. They are cleaned daily.

I hope by now you understand why I asked you to not take a flat. The challenges with living in a flat shared among friends are nothing but doable. But you're not in India. You're in a foreign country where it takes 10£ to get two visa photos clicked. You don't want to face these challenges (of making your own food, cleaning the house (oh btw they clean the room every weekday and change the covers every two weeks, here), cleaning the bathrooms, cleaning the dishes, managing groceries, need i go on?). My advise, take a student accommodation, focus only on the things you need to do and not how you clean those ;)

I think that covers it all. If I missed something or you have a question, please do ask me.

Flight

Short version: It doesn't matter which flight you travel via, make sure you get the best offer and try not to buy the whole round-trip tickets at once.

I flew via Etihad Airways, booked the tickets using ClearTrip two months prior to the session start date. I only booked this one flight so far from Delhi to London. The flight I took was divided in three phases: 4 hour flight to Abu Dhabi, 2 hours of lay-over at Abu Dhabi airport and 8 hour flight to Heathrow Airport, London. The flights were smooth and food was good. There was no problem with the luggage. Etihad airways allowed me to put upto 45kgs weight in my check-in baggage and upto 7kgs in my cabin bag. Etihad Airways has an app which I used to prebook my seat 24 hours prior to the flight. I got my boarding pass in hand for the second flight right there, but had to visit the check-in desk at the Indian airport to send my bags in and manually collect the boarding pass for the first flight.

Food

Short version: Eat the food in your accommodation whether you like it or not (it's a great saving tip, trust me). For the rest, read the full extract.

For all weekday lunches and weekend dinners when the accommodation canteen is closed, I usually get food from supermarkets or more like superstores. The following are the list of stores that you will be going to, priority vise:

  1. Tesco Metro/ Tesco Express (Both are same, Express is smaller/local version)
  2. Sainsbury/ Sainsbury Local
  3. Waitrose
  4. Mark's n Spencer (MnS)
Tesco is amazing for something called a "Meal Deal" wherein you can choose three items (from a list of options irrespective of their prices) and you'd only have to pay 3£ for the combo. They have other great offers as well. During night times usually, they reduce prices of stuff as well.

I don't go to these stores everytime nor am I asking you to. But it's the best way to budget while having a lot of options. The second most important place you'll find in London is the McDonald's. Their cheeseburgers are a hungry man's paradise. McDonald's is one of the cheapest options as well amongst Subway, KFC, Burger King, etc which are priced in a very similar way but are much better than what you get in India. There's a lot Starbucks and Costa's here as well, along with a very successful chain here known as Pret A Manger. They have really good hot beverages.

There are a lot of local cafes, pubs, restuarants around every corner. It's literally upto you to either like them or not. I've been to quite a few of them and I've loved some while some were just meh, money waste.

Luggage

Short version: The weight of luggage you can carry depends on the airline you travel through. Etihad airways permits 45kgs of check-in baggage and 7kgs in handbag.

The biggest question about packing for a very long trip is what to NOT take. So, my philosophy was simple. I asked myself "What all things will I regret NOT carrying" and I put those stuff in my bag first. I brought two big suitcases and one bagpack. My suitcases together weighed about 35kgs while my handbag weighed about 6kgs. The reason I left 10kgs of space off of my luggage is coz of the stuff I'll buy during my trip. If my luggage weighed even 1kgs more than the legal limit while coming back, I would either have to pay a large sum to extend the weight to be carried or I would have to throw some of my stuff away. A good way of packing for your trip is to mark those things in your checklist which you'd use and throw (like most of the toiletry stuff) or eatables. I knew the accessibility of food is convenient in London so I didn't carry a lot of food. I had 1 ready-to-eat meal for emergencies and 2 cup-noodles. Whilst a lot of my colleagues carried a lot of Indian sweets and ready-to-eats. Honestly, the one thing I don't want when I go out is "home-food". Travel in London

Short version: The cheapest means of traveling in London is walking. Followed by bus, underground (metro), Uber, Black Taxi, Fake Taxi, in the respective order.

You will be walking a lot unless you're rich.The first time I was going to travel through the Underground (that's that they call the metro here coz apparently it's underground. like woah. I'm sure the guy who named it would be drunk talking to his mate like "oi lad, what shuuwe name tha train below us?", 'you mean the train underground?', "UNDAGROUND!!"). [sorry] The first time I was going to travel in the underground, I didn't have the Oyster card (I'll get to it in a bit). So, I check the ticket price to the station I wanted to go to and it was 4.90£. One way. I was like "nope". Got an Oyster card made.

Oyster Card, is basically your Indian metro card but it works on buses here as well. You deposit a security for the card, get it topped up and use it in Underground stations and buses. The price for the Underground varies on the how far you're traveling just like in Delhi but it's not exactly the same. So, basically London is divided into zones. The innermost/central part of London is Zone 1 (my accommodation is in Zone 1). As you go outwards, the Zone number increases. I guess there are 5 or 6 Zones but seriously you won't ever be going there. Zone 3 is the most I've gone so far and it was a loooong way. London is huge. If you go across multiple zones, then you're charged more. I'll attach a photo of the prices down below. You can get Oyster card made from local general stores or more conveniently at the underground stations itself.

The reason I have mentioned buses so far along with Underground is coz you'll be traveling more in buses than Underground if you want to save money. So everytime you go in the Underground, the minimum charge is 2.50£. Everytime you go in bus, it's about 1-1.50£. Buses are fast af as well. And I'm talking about the double storey London buses. These buses go fast af. Everytime you board a bus, you present your card, just like you present it at the Underground gates.

Now the question of navigating through London and Underground/Bus routes. GOOGLE MAPS. This thing will save your life. It has everthing. It even shows how much time the next bus will arrive in. Download offline map of London as well. Also, keep your Location enabled at all point of time. Enable your data saver so background apps dont use off your mobile data but keep Location enabled. I keep my bluetooth enabled as well as it's readily connected to my watch, and if my phone were to go farther than a specific distance, my watch signals me that.

Now, I've merely travelled once in a local bus in India so I can't make comparisons. Here, there are designated bus stops corresponding to a number of buses. For, example the NottingHill Gate bus stop B, has 4 buses which arrive to it. Which buses are arriving to the particular stop is written on the board next to the bus stop seats along with a route map for those buses as well. If you're using Google Maps, you'll get everything there precisely.

A Hack I found: So when you're choosing a destination on Google Maps and then looking up directions to it from your location, go to the metro/Underground tab, press options and then you'll have the choice to select mode of transportation from Train/Bus/Tram. I usually select bus at this point and then the map only shows me the bus routes available.

Visa

Short version: Amity will make a big deal out of it raise your middle fingers up and chill TF down. Visa process for both UK and US takes a little bit of discipline but it's much easier than what Amity says.

I'll start off listing the documents you require for both the UK and US visa. Then I'll share my experience for the same.

Visa process for UK was a big deal since our invite letters had been delayed. I received my invite letter on the 10th of December 2018 while my flight was for 8th January. Literally everyone said that the visa takes atleast 15 "working days" to process and December is a month with a lot of holidays so it's better to get be on the safe side, pay extra and apply for an express visa (which costs 12k INR more). I didn't go for the express and eventually I found out EVERYONE else was wrong. But I didn't know that on 7th December when I was hurriedly filling up my visa form. A form that was suppsed to take 45-60mins is something I filled in 20mins somehow, lol.Got my appointment scheduled for the 11th in the first slot at 8am. 11th was Monday, my visa arrived on Friday. Only I know how excruciating those few days in between were. There was no certainty about when my visa would arrive. But yeah, the visa took 5 days to process.

So, the way it works for UK visa is that an agency by the name of "VFS Global" does the document collection process for the UK embassy. So when you apply for the UK visa, you fill the form through VFS Global and this organisation is solely responsible for collecting your documents and getting you the stamp on your passport. You get a list of documents that you need to present after you fill the visa form. But you need to make a folder for the same documents much before as these documents are responsible for getting you your invite letter from Birkbeck university. Invite letter basically states the details of the program you'll be attending, along with your name and other details. It's one of the most important documents. The document list that Amity asks is what the picture above shows. There is no interview process for UK visa, just biometrics and document collection. The Regular fee for the UK visa is about 9K INR, while the express fee is 22K INR. Urgent 1 day Visas can be done for about 1Lac INR.

US visa process is one of the most hyped things about the 3C program. Now, I don't know how it goes out in India but the process that took place in London, for all of us, was honestly, futile. They literally knew every detail about us since a law firm had been asked by the Adelphi (the university you'll be going to in the US) University's administration to brief the embassy about the 3C program. Most of us had to just say "yes" to verify the information that they were stating. What was supposed to be an interview turned out to be a general conversation, only thing missing was a cup of beer :) beer > coffee. A list of the documents asked by Amity for the invite letter has been attached as the image above.

You can't apply for the US visa before you apply for the UK one since you're going to UK first and then US. I was unable to apply for the US visa in India itself coz of insufficient time. But, now that I retrospect, I could've. Although I believe the process that took place here in London was much easier than what would've gone down in India. The fee structure for the US visa is bad. There's something called SEVIS. In a nutshell you have to pay a fee to the Department of Homeland Security to go through your details, check your background, etc., known as the SEVIS Fee. SEVIS Fee is 180$. Then the visa fee is separate which is 160$. So, basically you have to pay about 23K INR for the US visa which is kinda shitty.. The visa that you will be getting is a J1 visa.

Funny/Real story: Whilst I was waiting in line for the women affront to accomplish her interview, I could do nothing but eavesdrop. She was from an Italian origin, working under an American company in the States (obviously). For non-residents of the US, one needs a proper American contract to get a visa. This woman, did not have a proper one but she had been granted visas in the past and had traveled to the States (of America) a lot of times. As her visa was rejected due to an improper American contract she started protesting [I looked at the young man behind me and gasped "looks like we're gonna be here all day", to which he replied "IKR"] which lasted for about 10 minutes post which she gave up. As she turned around and was about to leave, she looked at me, smiled and said (and I quote), "Good luck with her". Referencing to the woman who took her interview. Contrary to her interview, mine went smoothly and I was done in under 2 mins. 2 mins. Done. Approved. US Visa. YAY Viva la USofA!!wootwoot. Weather

Short version: C O L D

London weather is unpredictable. Sun shines, it rains, it drizzles, it snows, sun shines, there's an overcast without rain, and repeat. The rain is such a bad bitch,that you can't figure out which direction it is coming from as it changes direction every other minute. It doesn't snow as much as one would imagine in London. I was under the impression that there'd be proper knee-level snow but no, that doesn't happen around here while the snowfall that does happen, is a beauty in itself
